Food is not only consumption—it is climate infrastructure.

It moves emissions, risk, and inequality through everyday urban systems.

Food is not only a consumer good—it is climate infrastructure. Across production, processing, distribution, and retail, food systems move carbon, price signals, and risk through urban networks. Yet these infrastructures are unevenly accessible: storage capacity, affordability, and retail geographies make climate and food insecurity spatially patterned. This project frames the city as the critical scale where global food–climate dynamics become governable and where everyday choices can aggregate into systemic change.

Food as climate infrastructure: how systemic flows translate into everyday levers.

Project Drawdown: Food systems are central levers for climate solutions.
NYC context: NYC recognizes food–climate connections through policy and procurement, but neighborhood conditions remain unequal.
Why city scale: Cities are where global flows become governable and everyday behavior can aggregate into systemic change.
