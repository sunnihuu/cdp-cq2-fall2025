Precedents & References

Precedents

Project Drawdown

Project Drawdown identifies food systems as one of the most powerful leverage points for climate action, linking diet shifts, food waste reduction, and land management to both emissions reduction and carbon removal. This project builds on Drawdown's global framework but shifts the scale of intervention—from planetary metrics to everyday urban behavior. Rather than focusing on abstract targets, it asks how food-climate logic can be translated into daily decision-making at the neighborhood level.

NYC Food & Climate Strategy

The NYC Food & Climate Strategy formally recognizes food as a core component of the city's climate response, with goals focused on reducing food-related emissions, eliminating food waste from landfills, and building a resilient and equitable food system. This project aligns with the Strategy's objectives, but operates at a different scale—prototyping behavioral and neighborhood-level systems that can complement municipal policy tools and support localized implementation.

Behavioral & Interface Design

Several behavioral and interface-based projects have explored how information design can influence food and climate-related choices, such as carbon labeling or menu-based nudging systems. These projects demonstrate that behavior can shift when information is made visible at the point of decision. This thesis extends this approach by embedding behavioral interfaces within a broader urban vulnerability framework, rather than treating behavior as an isolated individual choice.

Together, these precedents establish the policy, scientific, and behavioral foundations for this project, while highlighting the need for neighborhood-scale operational systems.

Annotated Bibliography

Project Drawdown. Drawdown: The Most Comprehensive Plan Ever Proposed to Reverse Global Warming.

Provides a global framework for climate solutions, identifying food systems as a top leverage point for emissions reduction and carbon removal.

NYC Mayor's Office of Food Policy. NYC Food & Climate Strategy.

Outlines New York City's policy approach to food-related emissions, waste reduction, and resilience, forming a key policy context for this project.

IPCC. Climate Change and Land.

Explains the relationship between land use, agriculture, food systems, and climate change at a global scale.

FAO. Food Systems and Climate Change.

Analyzes how food systems contribute to emissions and where mitigation opportunities exist.

Elizabeth Shove et al. The Dynamics of Social Practice.

Provides a theoretical basis for understanding everyday behavior as structured by systems rather than individual choice alone.

Thaler & Sunstein. Nudge.

Introduces behavioral decision frameworks relevant to food choice and policy design.

USDA. Household Food Security in the United States.

Offers metrics and definitions for food insecurity and household vulnerability.

NYC Open Data Portal. Food, Health, and Demographics Datasets.

Primary source for spatial and demographic data used in this project.

Data Sources & Provenance

Primary Data Sources

  • NYC Open Data (restaurant inspections, food infrastructure)
  • American Community Survey (poverty, household characteristics)
  • NYC Department of Health (food environment indicators)
  • Spatial boundary datasets (NTA, census tracts)

These datasets are publicly available and widely used in urban research. However, they reflect administrative definitions and reporting structures, which shape how food vulnerability is measured and represented. This project treats data not as neutral facts, but as situated indicators that require careful interpretation.

Materials & Sensors

This project is primarily data-driven and interface-based. It does not rely on physical sensors at this stage, but on spatial data analysis and behavioral interface prototyping.
